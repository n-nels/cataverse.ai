The development of structure–function relationships for metals supported on oxides has been beneficial for understanding their performance in energy applications. However, translating this knowledge into predicted synthesis outcomes is complicated by the limited understanding of how adsorbates affect metal atom diffusion and the structural transformation of metallic phases. To address this knowledge-to-synthesis gap, I propose leveraging a custom-built closed-loop multimodal spectroscopic platform, combined with multiscale modeling, to quantitatively monitor in-situ the real-time concentration of metal-carbonyl monomers during the nucleation and transformation of metastable (multi)metallic sub-nanometer clusters. The monomer disappearance kinetics will be used to derive continuous values for the metal-carbonyl chemical potential as metastable phases transform. In situ characterization tools will be used to correlate the chemical potential with the physical and chemical properties of transient metastable phases. This approach provides a framework for understanding how an expansive range of system parameters affect synthetic energy landscapes and structure outcomes for metals on oxides while promising to enable adaptive control and predictive design through the development of multivariable phase diagrams (Fig. 1).

The structural evolution of supported metals and alloys is driven by the system’s tendency to minimize its free energy. This process depends on differences in chemical potential of the metal atoms. While this relationship is well established,1 predicting how and where the metal atoms will relocate remains challenging due to the complex interplay of factors that influence the chemical potential of metal atoms supported on heterogeneous oxides. These factors include the metal identity and surface density, support composition and structure, and the distribution of metal coordination environments. This complexity is further amplified during chemical reactions, in which the formation of metal–adsorbate complexes introduces marked perturbations to the metal chemical potential, ultimately driving atom diffusion and undesired structural reorganization.2 The kinetics of metal–adsorbate monomer formation and disappearance caused by cluster nucleation provide information about the energy landscape, whereas adsorbates provide tunability to the metal atom chemical potential. This motivates efforts to elucidate nucleation and growth kinetics and the characterization of metastable phases to understand and ultimately control structural evolution.

Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF) statement: This project aims to advance an autonomous closed-loop materials synthesis, characterization, and performance evaluation platform that modulates the chemical potential of oxide-supported late transition metal atoms to adaptively control atom nucleation and provide operational stability to sub-two nanometer clusters.
Science & Technology Challenge: Can we algorithmically determine synthesis parameters for arbitrary oxide-supported metals that direct atom nucleation to achieve desired chemical and physical functionality while simultaneously developing a digital twin using real-time data to predicatively mitigate undesired structural changes (e.g., sintering) during operation?
Oxide-supported transition metal catalysts are crucial for energy conversion, chemical production, and environmental protection. However, their functional design mostly relies on a trial-and-error approach. This iterative process is currently the most efficient pathway to market due to the complex nature of these materials. Two main factors contribute to this complexity: (i) the spontaneous descent of supported metal atoms along local energy gradients during exposure to process conditions and (ii) a dearth of accessible in situ methods that quantify this energetic trajectory at a system level in real time with atomic resolution. These challenges lead to unpredictable structural evolution and the inability to adaptively direct materials towards the desired functionality.
The chemical potential of atomically dispersed transition metal atoms (adatoms) is well understood to determine catalyst structure and mediate its structural evolution during reactions through the formation of adatom-adsorbate complexes. However, there has been no systematic effort to quantify and manipulate the adatom-adsorbate chemical potential as a strategy to control structural attributes that confer desired chemical and physical functionalities. Consequently, a significant knowledge gap exists in understanding, predicting, and controlling catalyst structures at the atomic level for more efficient and sustainable chemical conversion technologies.
Research Approach: The closed-loop platform utilizes probe molecule vibrational spectroscopy for real time diagnostic feedback during materials synthesis and is complemented by Bayesian active learning for rapid parameter space exploration. We will use carbon monoxide (CO) as the probe molecule for two primary reasons: (i) CO adsorption onto site-isolated metal atoms produces a vibrational fingerprint that can be related to concentration, and (ii) the diffusion barrier of late transition metals is significantly lower upon CO adsorption, which promotes the precipitation of atoms into clusters. Initially, the CO probe will interrogate materials exclusively composed of atomically dispersed metals supported on refractory oxides, such as Pd/CeO2. The mobility and speciation (i.e., chemical potential) of the adatoms will be monitored and modulated in situ by controlling pretreatment and experimental conditions through the introduction of co-adsorbates at specific pressures and temperatures.
We will assess equilibrium cluster properties (e.g., site counting, sorption properties, chemical titration, etc.) in situ through pulse and steady state switching techniques using mass spectrometry (MS). Material performance will be evaluated in situ using a plug-flow reactor operating in parallel with the diagnostic cell by step switching from the synthetic feed to the reactant feed. Our integrated approach enables us to relate pretreatment and process conditions to the chemical potential of metal atoms, which in turn dictates structure and ultimately material performance—all within a single platform. We will benchmark the materials under equilibrium conditions, where the chemical potential is zero, using complimentary ex-situ analyses (e.g., XAS, STEM). The equilibrium states—defined by the synthetic pathway, characterization, and performance—represent phase boundaries between the adatoms that are in equilibrium with the attendant clusters. We can then use these state variables to construct phase diagrams and provide the foundation for enhanced predictive and control capabilities. It is important to note that metal clusters (< 1 nm) can be redispersed into isolated atoms by subjecting the material to intermediate-temperature oxidation conditions. This enables 24/7 continuous operation without the need for sample replacement, thereby accelerating materials discovery.
